Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)

Burning when peeing

Infection in the bladder or urinary tract, very common especially in women.

Burning sensation Frequent urge Cloudy urine
Common symptoms:
  • Burning or pain while urinating
  • Needing to pee very often, but only a little comes out
  • Cloudy, strong-smelling or slightly bloody urine
  • Lower belly discomfort
Basic prevention:
  • Drink enough water during the day
  • Do not hold urine for very long
  • Wipe from front to back after using the toilet
  • Pee soon after sexual activity
See a doctor urgently if:
  • You have fever, back pain, or vomiting
  • Symptoms are strong or do not improve within 1–2 days
Usually mild, can spread if ignored
Vaginal yeast infection

Intense itching or cottage-cheese discharge

Overgrowth of a natural fungus, often after antibiotics, stress, or moisture.

Thick discharge Redness Itching
Common symptoms:
  • White, thick discharge that looks like cottage cheese
  • Itching and burning in the vulva/vaginal area
  • Redness, swelling, or soreness
  • Pain during sex or while peeing
Basic prevention:
  • Keep the area clean and dry
  • Avoid very tight, synthetic underwear – choose cotton
  • Do not use scented soaps, sprays, or vaginal douches
See a doctor if:
  • It’s your first time and you are unsure of the cause
  • Symptoms keep coming back
  • There is strong smell, fever, or pelvic pain
Usually treatable with medicines
Bacterial vaginosis (BV)

Fishy smell or greyish discharge

Imbalance of natural vaginal bacteria, not a “dirty” problem and not an STI by itself.

Fishy odour Thin discharge Irritation
Common symptoms:
  • Thin grey/white discharge
  • Strong fishy smell, especially after sex
  • Mild itching or burning
Basic prevention:
  • Avoid vaginal douching and harsh products
  • Use condoms to reduce changes in vaginal bacteria
  • Change pads/liners regularly during periods
See a doctor if:
  • You are pregnant and notice these symptoms
  • Symptoms keep coming back or are very bothersome
Important to treat in pregnancy
Sexually transmitted infections (STIs)

Pain, sores, warts or unusual discharge

Infections passed through sexual contact (e.g., chlamydia, gonorrhoea, HPV, herpes).

Unprotected sex Sores/warts Pelvic pain
Possible warning signs:
  • Unusual discharge or bleeding between periods
  • Burning while peeing or pelvic pain
  • Blisters, warts, or sores on genitals
  • Pain during sex
Basic prevention:
  • Use condoms or protection during sex
  • Get regular check-ups if sexually active
  • HPV vaccination (as advised by a doctor)
See a doctor/clinic quickly if:
  • You notice any sores, warts, or unusual bleeding
  • You had unprotected sex with a new partner
Needs medical check & tests
PCOS & menstrual issues

Irregular periods or heavy bleeding

Hormone-related conditions that can affect periods, fertility, skin, and weight.

Skipped periods Acne / hair Cramps
Possible signs:
  • Periods coming very late, very often, or not at all
  • Very heavy bleeding or severe cramps
  • Increased facial/body hair, acne, or weight changes
Self-care & lifestyle:
  • Balanced diet and regular movement/exercise
  • Maintain healthy weight as advised by doctor
  • Good sleep and stress management
See a doctor if:
  • Your periods are irregular for several months
  • Pain is so strong you can’t do daily activities
Needs medical follow-up
Cervical health & screening

Pap smear & HPV awareness

Regular screening can detect early cell changes and reduce cervical cancer risk.

Pap test HPV vaccine Early detection
Why screening matters:
  • Can detect cell changes before they become serious
  • Some HPV infections have no symptoms but still cause changes
General guidance (varies by country):
  • Talk to your doctor about when to start Pap tests
  • Ask if HPV vaccination is right for you
See a doctor if:
  • You have bleeding after sex or between periods
  • You notice persistent pelvic pain or foul-smelling discharge
Screening saves lives
Prevention
Daily hygiene & protection

Small habits every day can reduce the risk of infections and discomfort. These tips are general; always follow your doctor’s advice first.

Daily hygiene checklist

Gentle care is better than over-cleaning. The vagina cleans itself. You only need to wash the outer area (vulva).

  1. Wash the outer area once a day
    Use lukewarm water or very mild, unscented soap on the vulva only. Do not insert soap inside.
  2. Wipe front to back
    After using the toilet, wipe from front to back to avoid spreading germs from anus to vagina/urethra.
  3. Change pads / tampons regularly
    During periods, change pads every 4–6 hours (or sooner if full). Avoid using one pad for the whole day or night.
  4. Choose breathable underwear
    Prefer cotton underwear; avoid staying long in wet clothes (swimwear, sweaty gym wear).
  5. Practice safer sex
    Use condoms, especially with new or multiple partners, and talk openly about STI testing.

Did you know?
Scented washes, vaginal steaming, and douching may disturb natural pH and actually increase infection risk. Simple water + gentle patience is usually best.
Self-care for comfort days

These ideas can support comfort but do not treat serious infections. Stop if anything makes your symptoms worse.

  • ✓ Warm (not hot) water compress on the lower belly for cramps.
  • ✓ Loose, soft cotton clothing to let the area breathe.
  • ✓ Drink enough water and include fruits, vegetables, and iron-rich foods in your diet.
  • ✓ Track your period and symptoms in a diary or app – helpful to show your doctor.
  • ✓ Simple relaxation: slow breathing, stretching, or light walks can ease stress around your cycle.

Warning signs
When you should see a doctor

If you are ever unsure, it is always okay to ask for medical help. Trust your body – if something feels “not normal” for you, get it checked.

Go to a doctor or emergency room if you notice:
  • • Very strong pain in the lower belly or pelvis that doesn’t get better with rest or pain medicine.
  • • Fever, chills, or feeling very weak along with vaginal symptoms.
  • • Bleeding after sex, between periods, or after menopause.
  • • Strong, foul-smelling discharge with pain or itching.
  • • Suspicion of pregnancy with pain or heavy bleeding.
In an emergency, do not wait for this website. Call your local emergency number or visit the nearest hospital. 🌸 You deserve gentle, respectful healthcare.
FAQ
Questions many women quietly ask

You are never “silly” or “too sensitive” for asking questions. If something worries you, it is valid.

Is vaginal discharge always a sign of infection? +

No. Some discharge is normal – it can change with your menstrual cycle and may be clear, white, or slightly sticky. Warning signs include: very strong smell, greenish/grey colour, cottage-cheese-like clumps, or discharge with pain, itching, or bleeding. In those cases, see a doctor for proper tests.

Can I wash inside the vagina to keep it extra clean? +

No douching is recommended. The vagina cleans itself naturally. Putting soaps, antiseptics, or water inside can disturb the natural bacteria and pH, and may cause infections or irritation. Only gently wash the outer area (vulva).

Is it safe to wash hair or bathe during periods? +

Yes. You can bathe or wash your hair during periods. In fact, warm baths can help relax cramps and improve comfort. Just change your pad/tampon/menstrual cup regularly and dry yourself properly.

How do I talk to a doctor if I feel shy? +

You can write your symptoms on paper or in your phone and show it to the doctor. Use simple words like “itching”, “burning”, “unusual smell” or “pain during sex”. A good doctor will listen without judgement. You can also ask for a female doctor if that makes you more comfortable (where available).

Can I use home remedies only instead of seeing a doctor? +

Mild discomfort can sometimes improve with better hygiene, loose clothing, and avoiding irritants. But home remedies should never replace medical care if you have strong symptoms, pain, fever, bleeding, or if symptoms keep coming back. Always seek professional help for serious or recurring problems.

Is everything I read online trustworthy? +

Not always. Use trusted health sources (hospitals, public health sites, recognised NGOs). This website is for general awareness and cannot replace a personalised medical opinion. For any doubt about your own body, a real doctor who can examine you is the best guide.
